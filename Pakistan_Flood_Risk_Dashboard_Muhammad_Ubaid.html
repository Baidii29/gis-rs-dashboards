Remote Sensing Methodology
This dashboard uses spectral indices derived from Landsat-8/9 and Sentinel-2 satellite imagery to map flood inundation extent, monitor soil moisture, assess vegetation health in flood-affected areas, and detect standing water across Pakistan's river plains. These are the standard indices used in international flood disaster assessment research.
NDWI
Normalized Difference Water Index — Flood Inundation Mapping
NDWI = (Green − NIR) / (Green + NIR)Sentinel-2: (B3 − B8) / (B3 + B8)  ·  Landsat-8: (B3 − B5) / (B3 + B5)
Pakistan Flood Season Average0.52
NDWI detects open water bodies by exploiting water's strong green reflectance and NIR absorption. Values above 0.3 indicate standing water. During flood events, NDWI maps the precise extent of inundation across the Indus floodplain, enabling district-level damage assessment.
Flood ApplicationUsed to map real-time flood inundation extent from Sentinel-2 imagery during monsoon events. Comparison with pre-flood NDWI baseline quantifies newly flooded areas — critical for emergency response prioritization and insurance damage assessment.
NDVI
Normalized Difference Vegetation Index — Agricultural Damage Assessment
NDVI = (NIR − Red) / (NIR + Red)Sentinel-2: (B8 − B4) / (B8 + B4)  ·  Landsat-8: (B5 − B4) / (B5 + B4)
Post-flood Crop Area Average0.18
NDVI measures vegetation density and health. Pre-flood values of 0.5–0.7 indicate healthy crops. Post-flood values dropping to 0.1–0.2 confirm crop destruction. Pakistan lost 3.6 million hectares of crops in 2022 — quantified precisely using multi-temporal NDVI change detection.
Flood ApplicationPre- vs post-flood NDVI comparison maps agricultural damage extent across Punjab and Sindh. In 2022, satellite NDVI analysis detected 45% reduction in Sindh's kharif crop cover within 2 weeks of peak inundation — enabling rapid humanitarian response planning.
MNDWI
Modified Normalized Difference Water Index — Urban Flood Detection
MNDWI = (Green − SWIR) / (Green + SWIR)Sentinel-2: (B3 − B11) / (B3 + B11)  ·  Landsat-8: (B3 − B6) / (B3 + B6)
Urban Flood Zone Average0.44
MNDWI is superior to NDWI in urban environments because it suppresses built-up land signals that can confuse water detection. Uses SWIR instead of NIR — critical for mapping flood inundation in cities like Sukkur, Larkana, and DG Khan where buildings interfere with standard NDWI.
Flood ApplicationUsed specifically for urban flood mapping in Sindh and Punjab cities. MNDWI correctly identified flooded streets and buildings in Sukkur during 2022 where standard NDWI gave false negatives due to mixed building/water pixels in 10m Sentinel-2 imagery.
NDSI / SAR
SAR Backscatter — Cloud-Penetrating Flood Mapping
Flood Detection via SAR Backscatter ChangeSentinel-1 C-band SAR · VV+VH polarization · 10m resolution
SAR Backscatter Difference (dB)−8.4 dB
Pakistan's monsoon floods are obscured by thick cloud cover — making optical satellites (Landsat, Sentinel-2) unusable during peak flooding. Sentinel-1 SAR (Synthetic Aperture Radar) penetrates clouds and rain to detect flood extent. Flooded surfaces show sharp backscatter decrease of 6–10 dB.
Flood ApplicationThe only reliable method for mapping active flood extent during Pakistan's monsoon season. In 2022, Sentinel-1 SAR imagery was used by UNOSAT to map 160,000 km² of inundated area across Sindh and Balochistan — directly informing UN emergency operations.
Satellite Platforms Used in Pakistan Flood Assessment

Scientific Background
Why Pakistan Floods — and Why It's Getting Worse
Pakistan is one of the most flood-prone countries on Earth. The convergence of the world's largest irrigation system, extreme monsoon rainfall, glacial meltwater from the HKH mountains, and rapid unplanned urbanization creates a perfect storm of flood vulnerability that is intensifying every decade as climate change disrupts the water cycle.
🌧️
What Causes Pakistan's Catastrophic Floods
  • The Indus River system drains 1.12 million km² — the largest contiguous irrigation system on Earth, carrying water from HKH glaciers to the Arabian Sea
  • Monsoon rainfall from the Indian Ocean delivers 60–70% of Pakistan's annual rainfall in just 90 days (July–September), overwhelming river capacity
  • Glacial meltwater from 7,253 HKH glaciers adds runoff on top of monsoon rain — a double hazard unique to Pakistan
  • Deforestation of Indus catchment watersheds has reduced soil absorption capacity by an estimated 40% since 1990, increasing surface runoff
  • Unplanned urban expansion into natural floodplains has put millions of homes directly in flood paths that were historically unpopulated
  • The 2022 event was declared a "climate catastrophe" by the UN — Pakistan contributed less than 1% of global emissions yet suffered one of the worst climate disasters in history
📊
The Vulnerability Framework — How Districts Are Scored
  • Hazard Exposure (40%): Historical flood frequency, proximity to major rivers, elevation above flood plain, rainfall intensity records
  • Population Sensitivity (25%): Population density in flood zones, percentage of population below poverty line, proportion of mud/brick homes
  • Infrastructure Capacity (20%): Road connectivity for evacuation, hospital and shelter availability, early warning system coverage
  • Agricultural Dependency (15%): Proportion of population dependent on flood-vulnerable crops — critical for Sindh and southern Punjab
  • Districts scoring above 75 on the composite FVI are classified Extreme — requiring permanent early warning infrastructure
  • Data is updated annually using NDMA damage reports, PMD rainfall records, and Sentinel-2 flood inundation mapping
Types of Floods Affecting Pakistan
🌧️
Riverine Floods
Caused by prolonged monsoon rainfall exceeding Indus system capacity. Affects Punjab, Sindh plains. Slow-onset but massive inundation lasting weeks to months
⚡
Flash Floods
Sudden intense rainfall in KPK and Balochistan mountain catchments. Extremely fast onset — communities have minutes to hours of warning. Highly destructive
🏔️
GLOF Floods
Glacial Lake Outburst Floods from HKH glaciers releasing into Indus tributaries. Adds dangerous meltwater pulse on top of monsoon rainfall
🌊
Urban Floods
Overwhelmed drainage systems in Karachi, Lahore, Peshawar during intense rainfall events. Worsened by rapid unplanned urban growth onto natural drainage corridors
